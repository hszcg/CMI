Introduction to Algorithmic Randomness

Tuesday January 1, 2013

Speaker: George Barmpalias

Place: 1-222 FIT Building, Time: 12 pm

  • Abstract

    • In this short 3-lecture course I will discuss the subject of Algorithmic randomness and in particular the mathematical theory that stems from the following questions and notions:

    - What do we mean by randomness?
    - how can we define randomness?
    - Focus on binary sequences: finite/infinite
    - Randomness/coding information relationships
    - The other end: anti-randomness
    - Which sequence is more random?

    I will start with the foundations of algorithmic randomness (Kolmogorov, Solomonoff, Levin, Chaitin) and proceed to a number of interesting results, most of them quite recent. I will not always follow the historical order of events and the style will be rather informal, like telling a story and focussing on the bits I personally find interesting and know more about. I will not talk about pseudo-randomness, but rather about stronger forms of randomness (quite a few of them). I will talk about randomness of binary strings and infinite binary sequences, and focus on the latter. Randomness of other mathematical objects can be defined accordingly through standard coding.

    In the first lecture I will focus on motivating and presenting the mathematical foundations of the theory, along with occasional key results. The theme of the second lecture will be computational lowness (or anti-randomness) which is a rather popular theme in this area nowadays. In the final lecture I will talk about measures of complexity that compare infinite sequences according to how random they are.

    I will try to keep the lectures reasonably self-contained so that people can drop in after having missed a lecture.

    Some encyclopaedic texts on the subject are:

    http://www.amazon.com/Computability-Randomness-Oxford-Logic-Guides/dp/0199652600

    http://www.amazon.com/Algorithmic-Randomness-Complexity-Applications-Computability/dp/0387955674

    http://www.amazon.com/Introduction-Kolmogorov-Complexity-Applications-Computer/dp/0387339981

    A few of the results I will present are too recent to be found in these books, but references to papers will be given upon request.

  • Bio

    • I got my PhD from the University of Leeds in 2004. I have worked in Computability theory and algorithmic randomness and have published 50 articles in journals and conference proceedings. I have worked and taught in the University of Leeds, Victoria university of Wellington in New Zealand, University of Amsterdam and currently I work in the Chinese Academy of Sciences, Institute of Software. My new interest is the study of probabilistic models of networks and the associated phase transitions.
